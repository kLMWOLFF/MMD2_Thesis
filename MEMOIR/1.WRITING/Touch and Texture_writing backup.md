Introduction
This thesis is an investigation on touch; specifically on how we experiment touch through digital experiences. It examines how we engage with, extend, and transform our sense of touch (and its affordances, both digital and physical) in order to explore new forms of embodiment within hybrid spaces. The project aims to construct a mapping of Touch — a conceptual and sensory framework that helps to situate and make sense of what it is and how it operates as a system of relations that defines how we encounter and make sense of our surroundings. 
This work positions itself as an ethnographic research on present and future tactile possibilities, methodologically drawing on Sarah Pink’s sensory ethnography (2015), which proposes that ethnographic knowledge is generated through multisensory engagement with the world. Pink’s approach suggests that sensory ethnography is not simply about describing experience but about doing and feeling it as an embodied, performative practice of knowing. This framework is here adapted to a digital perspective, examining how sensory knowledge is constructed through technologically mediated forms of touch, and how these experiences shape our understanding of self, materiality, and presence.
Touch operates within a multisensory system, composed of several organs and perceptual modalities. It coexists in a complex network with other sensory “universes” (sight, sound, smell, taste) all overlapping in continuous movement while offering distinct yet complementary ways of apprehending the world, forming what could be described as a constellation of perceptions. Through our hybrid encounters with physical materiality and virtual fluidity and abstractions, we found new ways of mediating and extending our touch abilities to reach, feel, and act within spaces that exist beyond direct physical contact. Through these mediations, we cultivate what could be described as an extended tactile self: a mode of sensing and engaging with realities outside the immediate boundaries of the body.
Touch can be understood as the foundational sense through which the body encounters and gives meaning to the world. For Condillac (Traité des sensations, 1754), it is the origin of all knowledge of external reality — the act that differentiates self from others and teaches the remaining senses how to situate their perceptions in space. For Merleau-Ponty (Phenomenology of Perception, 1945), touch reveals the body’s dual nature as both subject and object: the one who touches is also touched. This reciprocity establishes the body’s role as the medium through which existence becomes tangible. Extending these ideas into the digital sphere, we will see how digital interaction remains anchored in tactile experience: the screen, sensor, or interface becomes an extension of the body’s motor intentionality, producing new embodied gestures and sensibilities. In this sense, touch persists as both epistemic and relational: a way of knowing and of being affected by the world — whether physical or virtual.

This thesis inquiry into how people imagine and sense the future through their bodies. Analysing the notion of designed embodiment — the intentional creation of environments that allow others to feel similarly emplaced. These designed environments emerge from trajectories, sensations, and actions woven into new ethnographic places, where participants inhabit and reconstruct meaning through tactile experience.
Following Douglas Porteous’s concept of sensory scapes (such as smellscapes or soundscapes) this work proposes the idea of a touchscape: a sensory and material landscape composed of tactile relations, digital interfaces, and embodied gestures. Mapping touchscapes involves tracing how individuals expand their sensory capacities through tools and technologies that extend the body’s reach. In the context of digital interaction, touch becomes both an input and a metaphor. The tactile screen, the responsive surface, and the haptic feedback mechanism redefine what it means to make contact. Through these interfaces, we construct new forms of spatial awareness and emotional connection, blurring the boundaries between the physical and the virtual.

Chapter 1 - State of the Art: Touch and Texture
The first chapter of this thesis is devoted to setting the foundations of the two main themes we’ll be working with: Touch and Texture. Both are extremely banal concepts, reason why they tend to become invisible and set autonomously at the background of our interactions. That’s also the reason why I felt the need to start this study by bringing forth their definitions and examining their tensions and motivations. If we have the aspiration to design Touch and Materiality, it’s vital to master their fundamentals first.
I start by addressing Touch, for which I go as far as analyzing Condillac’s and Merleau-Ponty’s works on sensations and perception. These theories remount from a couple of decades to a couple of centuries ago and the reason they were chosen was, first, because of the strength and depth of their review and, second, so we could have a « pure » look over Touch and its semiotics before addressing the gradually digitalization of our interactions. After understanding how our senses play in our body and complement each other, we will slowly make our way to their abstraction to proper concepts and perceptions that can then be translated into the digital space.
As for our second concept, Texture, I start by borrowing definitions from visual arts and fashion to anchor it first in the physical space, the same way it was done with Touch. The choice was done not only because those constitute my background in materiality, but also because both fields have in texture their default working material. After that I go into the definitions around Texture and Material in the digital space, specially in a 3D perspective. Since many logics and nomenclatures get updated within this passage from a physical to a digital rationality, juxtaposing both vocabularies will help precise their differences and refer more clearly to needed concepts in the next chapters.

The human body contains five classical senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste and touch. Those are more than just sensory organs or abilities to detect things; for both Condillac in Traité des Sensations (1754) and Merleau-Ponty in Phenomenology of Perception (1945) those are the way through which we exist as beings, since it's  thanks to our sensory capacities that our body is capable of interacting and gaining perspective on the world. Both philosophers also defend the unique and privileged position occupied by the sense of touch as the responsible for the link inserting the body into a space. For Merleau-Ponty, that demonstrates the body's unique status of being simultaneously a subject (the toucher) and an object (the touched). He uses the example of two hands touching each other to explain this phenomenon of "double sensations": "If I touch my right hand with my left hand while my right hand is touching an object, there is only one hand, strictly speaking, that touches. Always escaping totalization, my body is not merely a permanent object; it is 'that by which there are objects'" (1). Even though one body, the hand that touches and the hand that is touched cannot perfectly merge; they remain distinct roles that continually exchange between activeness and passiveness. To Merleau-Ponty this way through which the body experiences touch is what he calls the "flesh"- the texture of existing as a being.
Condillac on the other side, views touch as the foundational sense for true knowledge of the external world. In his Traité des Sensations (1754), he compares how we assimilate information coming from each sense using the metaphor of a statute that, coming to life, is limited to experiencing one sense at a time. Through such a thought experiment, he demonstrates that the sense of touch is the first one to allow the judgement of external objects as independent from our own body. Via its movements and the experience of solidity from its own body, at first, then from others existing outside itself, the statue gains the tactile ideas of extension, figure, movement, distance, and situation that can help it differentiate itself and everything it comes into touch with. As we’ll be seeing in more detail later in this chapter, these same tactile inputs will also be useful to us in digital spaces, helping understand the extent of our digital bodies and determine how to interact with digital objects. Once the statue has established the reality of this double internal/external existence and its sensations evolve to more complex, mixed-senses combinations, it's Touch that steps in to teach each one of those how to judge the external objects it already knows. It helps them link their primal sensations (sight -color, hearing - sound, smell - odor, taste- flavor) with the spatial judgments derived from touch (solidity, hardness, moisture, weight, temperature, distance, position, shape, size, movement) making them able to discern and interact with space and other bodies. 
If we grab a river stone, for example. The moment it settles in our palm, we can feel how cool and smooth it is, how different from our skin and how it does not respond to touch as another part of our own body would. We let it weigh in our hand, feeling the satisfying heft; it’s heavy for its size and the oval shape, perfectly rounded, just makes us want to run it over our fingers, mapping the contours of the stone, discovering every detail of its surface, even the small sharp incision in one edge. All those are tactile qualities, things we can infer about the object in our possession without the need of any other sense. We bring it closer to our eyes now. We see a dark, charcoal grey with a glassy sheen coat that reflects the sunlight, everywhere except for one thin, darker line, where a little shadow lingers. A small dot appears and starts slowly expanding, changing its color to a lighter and duller tone of grey, making also the sunlight reflections lose strength. Colors, lights and shadows are qualities from the sight. Taken isolated though, without the first lessons taught us by touch, they seem very abstract and unclear. It would be impossible to infer that the little dark line with a shadow is an indication of the depth of the incision on one of its edges. We also wouldn’t be able to compare the temperature and moisture in its surface to understand that the shiny coat implied the humidity still lingering from the river, disappearing as the rock dried. 
Condillac argues that the eye itself is naturally incapable of seeing distance, position, shape, size, or movement. Sight acquires these abilities through the instructions given by Touch as its primary sensations are only capable of transmitting the notion of a continuous surface without limits of distinctions between objects or shapes. Through the tutoring of touch, sight becomes a spatial sense and progressively fuses visual impressions and tactile judgements: by moving our hand back and forth, our sight can correlate the shrinking or growing size of the stone with the actual distance it is from us; by touching its curves, we guide the eye to discern the corresponding light and shadow patterns that associates to it texture and shape. The eye also learns how to identify an object is moving, as touch enables it to follow the stone while we move our hand, linking the visual displacement to the idea of movement. The result is that the eye becomes the most discerning and efficient sense, even to the point where sight's judgments are often mistakenly attributed to it alone instead of actually judgments of touch that have become habits of sight.
The same logic can also be applied to the other senses. If we bring the stone even closer to us than before, we are suddenly invaded by the scent of wet earth and dusty minerals. Touch teaches smell and taste about spatial awareness by tackling the intensity of an odor in comparison to the distance it is from us, as well as how to flavor textures, shapes, moisture and temperature; while it also connects those experiences to pleasure and displeasure sensations. The earth, minerals and freshness scents tell us about the moisture and coolness, how close we are from the water, the probability of trapping our feet in the soft mud close by. We hear a dull and deep "clack" as a second stone is added to the first one. According to touch, this informs us about their solidity. By agitating our hand closer and further away from our body, we can experience the sound sensation more or less vividly, determining the actual distance and position of both rocks in space. As they suddenly slip from our hand, we can hear the clattering of both rocks hitting multiple other ones as they bounce far from us. The comparison between the existence and absence of sounds (silence) can define the presence, position and movement of bodies, determining how many other beings there are, the space between them and, when the same sound is successively repeated from different positions, the path it used to move through space.
In the physical world, this interweaving between senses becomes natural after we learn how to correlate each primary sensation. We depend on it to confirm our first impressions and form the bigger picture of a situation: a cool, smooth heft + shiny charcoal grey + wet earth scent + clacking sound = riverbench stone. Synesthesia, this ability of translating qualities from one sense to the another, is also explored in digital interactions, cues redundancy speed and solidifies our interpretation, helping us to make faster decisions and relate more easily to interfaces. In a video game, for example, a faint, low hum of water and the gentle vibration of the controller might indicate we are close to a river. If the controller's rumble intensifies as we move our digital hands, we might infer that we are holding something on them; a solid object, probably somewhat weighty. If that impression is followed by a dull, deep clacking, it corroborates that first impression and makes us understand there are in fact two objects clashing in our palm. We see the dark objects in our hands, slick with moisture, they catch the faint light in two sharp glints. Beyond, a fast-moving river is visible, its surface churning and silvered. The banks are steep and lined with thick, blurred vegetation. The scene is dark, with cold colors and a mist is floating on top of the river. We can almost feel in the air the thick, damp smell of water mixing with the scent of wet clay, rock and the fresh pine needles from the forest we can see after the riverbench. 
The logic being used in physical and digital to bring those senses connections and help us interpret our surroundings is very similar. The main difference remains in the fact that, while in the physical the other senses are led by and come together around touch, in the digital that primacy belongs to sight.
Condillac's position on the physical authority of touch also reflects Merleau-Ponty’s concept of the habit as an embodied skill. For him, a habit is not a mechanical repetition, but a knowledge the body acquires. When the eye, for example, learns distance, this means the body has absorbed a new way of being in the world. This is the essence behind his concept of embodied cognition: the idea that our perception of ourselves depends on the body's physical interactions with the environment. Building spatial knowledge, in  Merleau-Ponty’s vision, is about perfecting the motor intentionality of the body, a tactile knowledge that allows us to respond instantaneously to the world without the need for rationally processing it.
Condillac endorses the Merleau-Ponty concept of embodied cognition as a sensory and spatial experience, but both authors differ in their understanding of the position occupied by the body. The first believes that the body is a source of sensory inputs through which all knowledge from the external world is received and then organized into concepts. This treats the body and the sensations evoked by touch as not continuous: unless we run our touch through all the space in between two points of a same object, we are unable to understand those two points as parts of the same being. For Meraleau-Ponty, the body is not a tool for assembling sensory data; the environment and other bodies, together, are the very texture of being-in-the-world, where the one who touches is also touched. For him, we don’t have a body, we are a body. The contrast between those theories is important to help us compare physical and digital logic concerning touch and textures. When considering our physical interactions, there is not much difference between a being and its body, the same way of a matter and a material; those are synonyms and indivisible from one another. When we go into digital environments though, most of our interactions are guided by ideas: simulations that access senses data in order to build our embodiment as digital beings.
In order to create such a data collection of tactile ideas - an alphabet of Touch we could call - we need to isolate and abstract the properties of bodies, specially variations between tactile qualities such as solidity and fluidity, hardness and softness. Those contrasts are the tactile vocabulary that allow us to map and build our understanding of digital bodies and materials. According to Condillac, sensations come from the interaction with other sensible bodies and exist out of ourselves. In that case we can detach their qualities from their original beings and configurations and form abstractions, applying those to different bodies. These isolated concepts are called in Traité des Sensations (1754) as ideas, the memory of a sensation, touched with our body and made autonomous. Merleau-Ponty also develops the notion of tactile memory although according to him, the body retains not abstracted ideas but motor habits — physical memories of movements that shape how new perceptions are understood. Through the perspective of his late work (The Visible and the Invisible, 1968), he treats tactile and the digital as different scales of embodiment; defending the digital body might be read as a new modality of flesh — an expanded field of double-relations where one can touch and be touched through images, surfaces, materials and data.
Mark B.N. Hansen also addresses this “becoming virtual” of the body in his work, Bodies in Code (2006), challenging both Condillac's detached body and Merleau-Ponty's "Flesh". While still following this later premise that body and being are the same thing, he shifts the focus to how this "lived body" is fundamentally changed by the digital environment, becoming something else. Hansen is concerned with how the body experiences and adapts to the digital, affecting the way we understand embodiment. He suggests, for example, that during this transition, we end up developing new sets of digital vocabulary, containing both new motor habits and new conceptual ideas, which mix direct translations from physical properties to new virtual ways of touch, influenced by code and data. That’s very similar to what we observed from our analysis of the physical and digital river stone before: in one, the motor habits of grab, run over fingers, cradle in our palm, move closer and further from the body and the ideas of coolness, smoothness, heft and shape built the physical tactility of the stone; in the other, the motor habits of pressing (screen/button), swiping, tilting (joystick/screen/controller), vibrating and the ideas of solidity, weight, temperature and moisture built its digital counterpart.
The primordial nature of tactility makes touch a necessary bridge to link the physical body to the digital interface, preventing the digital from becoming purely abstract or immaterial. Hansen's theory, just like Condillac’s and Merleau-Ponty’s, addresses the role of touch as a spatial sense, defending its importance in understanding the digital environment. He argues that there is no pure cyberspace divorced from our physical existence: every digital interaction is already a mixed one, anchored in the physical tactility. This hybridility is what makes the digital not a space where the body's rules are abandoned, but one where we can adapt and acquire new embodiment thanks to different sensorial habits and ideas - new tactile qualities that become the sensory manifestations of our digital bodies. This novel "touch" orientates and gives us agency allowing us not to simply perceive media, but act through it. Digital interfaces (like screens, mice, touchpads) reveal themselves to be not just passive tools, but extensions that reshape our body's motor intentionality, building us new sets of skills and reflexes (swiping, tapping, scrolling, pushing, leaning, specific buttons combinations). 
A further analysis from this perception of media through the point of view of our senses can be found in Laura U. Marks work Touch: Sensuous Theory and Multisensory Media (2002). In it, she defends that human communication begins in materiality, through the symbolic appropriation of firstness (immediate signs, perceived by the body) and thirdness (abstraction of these signs - their representation, perceived by memory and culture) and supporting the idea of a multisensory media experience. She starts by addressing the concept of synesthesia, describing such as a translation of embodiment that refreshes our perception of an event (be it physical or digital). For her, being able to transmit an experience means to trace its symbolic meaning as well as its materiality history through different sensorium perceptions. 
Marks also approaches the term haptics, central to her work, describing it as a perception that combines tactility, movement, and proprioceptive functions to allow us experience touch both on the surface and on the inside of our bodies. For her, haptics in media can be experienced in different formats, not all dependent on physical touch itself. The constant flow between touch and vision (haptic and optical) establishes what she calls our two main ways of seeing: Optical Visuality and Haptic Visuality. The author explains the first as the purely visual and practical way of seeing: the vision that maintains a certain distance from its subject and understands it in a detached, symbolic way, blinding us to the richness of the sensory experience. Haptic visuality, on the other hand, refers to an embodied and material vision that uses the eyes "like organs of touch" and engages the whole body in the process of seeing. This can be found in the work « Otiosi » from Z1 studio, described by Camile De Dieu: « We did a digitalization of a forest as part of a music project, and it was about creating an archive of a place that might one day disappear. In the way things move, one could imagine feeling the wind, for example. In that case, it was purely visual, but we still make those connections in memory ». 
For Marks, haptic and optical visualities are not exclusive, but exist as two sides of one another. She defends that learning how to touch an object or media, means to reach out for its unique network of material and symbological connections, through which sight is constantly influenced by the potential of touch. It's the symbiosis between sensory faculties, physical body and the interfaces that extends our senses into the virtual space that establishes for her, our true virtual embodiment.
The exploration of touch through Condillac, Merleau-Ponty, Hansen, and Marks reveals touch as a connective sense through which we are able to understand space, and develop embodied knowledge. In physical experiences, tactility guides other senses to form coherent spatial judgments, while in digital environments sight leads the other sensory systems by relying on tactile memories and motor habits to sustain a sense of embodiment. This raises a central question for digital materiality: how are tactile qualities communicated when physical contact becomes mediated by vision? The next section turns to texture, one of the primary expressions of touch, to examine how surface qualities translate sensory expectations and by doing so build bodies and materials.
TEXTURES/MATERIALS
Texture is the element directly associated with Touch - a group of properties that define the way something feels. When seen from the point of view of visual arts, is the quality both inherent to a material surface and that created or intensified by the artistic work with the intention of evoking a specific tactile feeling and emotion associated to it. Among arts subdivisions, sculpture is the field responsible for directly manipulating and playing with materiality and its textures: a sculptor can give a block of marble the impression of a soft, pliable flesh or turn a malleable piece of clay into a hard plank of wood.
In fashion, texture is closely related to the creation of patterns. Raw materials (fibers) are made into complex surfaces by using different techniques to arrange them into structured lines, loops and diagonals. This material weaving in itself is already capable of generating a new tactility, but fashion designers also work over the finished surface with cuts, sews, folds, embroidery and other techniques in order to generate textures on top of textures, in the same way sculptors deform a material surface to create new touchable perceptions. 
Independent of the field they’re being applied to, textures can present a number of different qualities. These are usually presented in a dialectical way, through pairs that help define each other by their opposition such as rough - smooth, soft - hard, sticky - dry, slippery - grippy, sleek - porous, rigid - flexible, warm - cool, fuzzy - pricky, moist - dry. Those adjectives are naturally referred to when describing the tactile qualities of how something feels for us and many are imported to define the configurations of digital materials as well.
When thinking in terms of materiality, digital and physical objects differentiate from each other in a primary way. If we take a 3D object as an example, by analysing it we can see it is composed of two things: a Matter - the 3D object itself - made of vertices, lines and faces(1) to which we give the name of mesh; and a Material - the 3D object looks and behaviors - composed of images and sets of parameters. Imagine a wooden cube, for example: Its matter is the cube with 6 faces that form its shape, while its material is the combination of wooden appearance and the rigidity, density and weight of that wood; basically, the way it will behave to physics simulation, like gravity. The matter defines the shape of the object and the position it occupies in the space but all the information on how it will take up this place, its interactions with the environment, other objects and the reaction to manipulation, are defined by its material. When we digitally touch an object, it's with their matter we come in direct contact with, but it is their material we see and that responds to our interaction. When asked about the way she saw this splitting between matter and material, Holly New, Founder & CEO of STUDIOOFNEW, commented:
When you have just the 3D model, the object looks very conceptual. Then as soon as you add a texture, people start going “Oh, ok. Now I understand and see all coming together”. So I think texture is really the way of communicating to people. I would say textures are realism, they bring the next level of association to a digital object. They become crucial in the design process the more we work with 3D and materials, because textures add the next level to it, make a mesh suddenly become a human or something else. 
A material is the substance from which things can be made. For physical reality, it is the matter that gives form, appearance, texture, and behavior to an object (meaning that all of those are one single thing). In the digital, however, a material is the appearance and behavior of an object (it does not contain matter and form, but complements it). Because of that separation, digital textures have a slightly different definition than their physical counterparts as well. To begin with, in digital a texture refers only to the visuals of an object. We can have, as in our last example, a wooden box that melts like water or inflates and flies like a balloon. The way a material looks (its texture) and the way it behaves (dynamic physical qualities) are different things entirely(2). This also changes the way we feel digital materials. How would you define the feel of a wooden melting cube? Is it rigid or flexible? soft or hard? moist or dry? The tactile qualities are still present in their pairing oppositions but they are not fixed to a specific surface; a same texture can be assigned to different and even opposing qualities. That’s what Marie-Laure Ryan describes In her work Narrative as Virtual Reality (2001)(3), as arbitrary physics. She cites Jaron Lanier “blowing” landscapes (4) and the way in digital reality (specially VR in her analysis) we could build synaesthetic relations through which “sounds have colors, colors have smells, smells have taste, and words acquire a body of their own”(5).
Digital materials have another important characteristic: in order to achieve more realistic and detailed visual properties, they developed a way of separating and reading different visual qualities independently. Going back to our wooden cube, this means that the wooden color, roughness, shining and relief size between grains are each a specific quality, assigned to a different texture. That way, a digital texture is not a group of properties showing how something should feel; but each one of those properties, independently. A single digital material can be composed of multiple textures (presented as image files), each one specialized in transmitting one specific visual cue to it. For our 3D cube, this means four textures (color - color gradient of lighter and darker wood grains, roughness - how light spreads and reflects, making varnished areas look slick and unsanded areas look matte, specular - the strength and amount of reflected light in varnished areas,  and normal - the illusion of fine detail like grain and scratches) spreading around it as layered coats in order to transmit the impression of a wooden material. This separation and fragmentation of haptic images into packets of information, make them into a system of signs that convey the impression of materiality by abstract ideas.
Marie-Laure Ryan (2001) writes on the connections between the physical and the virtual, understanding the digital reality as another step in the subjectification (or virtualisation) of our physical concepts of reality. She digs into image representation theories and try to define if images' evolution from physical representation to virtual autonomous are a sign we are abandoning our ties with the Real in favor of an illusion (Jean Baudrillard, 1981) (6); or if that virtualisation actually bring us closer to the “True Nature of the image” (Pierre Lévy, 1988) (7). From the first point of view, virtual would be the transformation of all reality, from our gestures to the material substance of the world, into pure information, the Fake over the Real. From the second, virtual does not oppose the real, but nourishes it. It is another way of being that expands our creativity and makes it possible to envision new futures and meanings beneath the banal of our physical reality. 
The digital fragmentation of texture into visual concepts opens us a new relationship with materiality. Digital media introduces arbitrary physics and multi-layered sensorium cues that expand creative possibilities and push us to rethink how we feel and understand materials. As Marie-Laure Ryan and Pierre Lévy propose, the virtual does not replace the real but transforms and nourishes it, revealing new forms of embodiment through the feedback loops created by both. Touch remains the link between those, anchoring us in their realities through embodiment and reaching through the impressions of solidity and the tactile qualities of textures. Marks analyzes our perception of media through the point of view of our senses, defending that human communication begins in materiality, through the symbolic appropriation of firstness (immediate signs, perceived by the body) and thirdness (abstraction of these signs - their representation, perceived by memory and culture) and supporting the idea of a multisensory media experience. She starts by addressing the concept of synesthesia, the ability of translating qualities from one sense to the another, describing such as a translation of embodiment that gets to actualize our perception of an event, be it physical or digital. The author unfolds the term yet by defining Haptic images and Haptic look. The first, are abstract images dominated by materiality (rough, grainy surfaces; cracked plaster; worn fabric) in which elements invite the eyes to skim the surface, tracing the textures more than the figures of the physical environment through close, surface-oriented looks, simulating the action of touching. It creates a unified field of view over a surface, inviting a bodily relationship between the viewer and the image. As for the haptic look, she defines it as a caressing gaze, in which the eye lingers over several surfaces instead of being pulled by one specific point. It's a look that rests on the surface of the texture instead of plunging into the depth of the figures and narratives, being more inclined to move than to focus, as it embodies the touch nature of spatial recognition. Whether evoked by imagination or physical contact, touch is the sense that links those by the impressions of solidity and resistance it conveys, both fundamental to achieve a sense of reality. This notion of embodiment is what determines the effectiveness of an environment, particularly in digital space; and it depends on the fact that that space recognizes our presence and reacts to it. As Ryan explains, referencing Thomas Sheridan’s “Musings”, the experience of presence and belonging depends on three variables: lifelikeness of the display (realism), the ability to explore an environment (mobility), and the ability to change it (interaction). She also works with Merleau-Ponty’s notion of embodiment saying that it is through our body and touch that we build a sense of others presence: 
«The ultimate test of the material existence of things is the ability to perceive them under many angles, to manipulate them, and to feel their resistance. When my actual body cannot walk around an object or grab and lift it, it is the knowledge that my virtual body could do so that gives me a sense of its shape, volume, and materiality. Whether actual or virtual, objects are thus present to me because my actual or virtual body can interact with them. In the case of an image, for instance, effects of texture and shading invite the viewer to touch the picture in imagination, thereby creating the corporeal relation that tells her, This is a real, solid, three-dimensional object that belongs to my world. » (9)
As stated by Ryan: “While actualization is the invention of a concrete solution to answer a need, virtualization is a return from the solution to the original problem”(8). This would make designing , digital and physical could be seen as two sides of the same coin as well, working together in a feedback relationship of constant actualization and virtualisation in order to allows creativity to survive.

Chapter 2 - Field Research: Digital Textures
In the last chapter, we approached the definitions of Touch and Texture, through which embodiment and interactions with surrounding objects take place in both physical and digital experiences. We also approached how materials change from unified concepts on the first one, to a network combination of haptic and other sensorium qualities, on the second. If the notion of digital materials is built from fragments of perception, then understanding how they operate in practice requires looking at the environments where they are experienced. Textures in digital contexts are not just visual surfaces; they mediate interactions between users and systems. Whether we touch a screen, navigate a virtual world, or manipulate a digital twin of a real object, textures become interfaces that guide our actions and communicate possibilities or reactions to our contact.
To examine how textures are implemented and construct meaning in digital environments, this chapter will focus on three case studies, chosen from across different domains of everyday contact with the digital: a video game, a software interface, and a design application built around digital twins. The examples were selected to cover different usage scenarios and compare how touch and materials are explored through them. The two main criteria applied to their curation were their interactivity and their usage by a large number of users. Each example demonstrates a distinct way of using materials as communicative tools, from their visual textures to their behaviors.
The first case, Minecraft (2011), shows how an extremely simple visual system can generate rich tactile experiences in virtual environments. The second, Apple’s Liquid Glass (2025), explores a digital material designed to behave like a new substance shaped by touch. The third, IKEA Kreativ (2022), reveals the tensions and possibilities in connecting digital and physical materiality. Together, these case studies illustrate the spectrum of digital texturing practices and how they shape users’ capacity to feel the virtual space.

MINECRAFT
Minecraft is one of the most popular video games in the world with over 300 million copies sold worldwide and over 204 million monthly active players (2023). The game is a sandbox where players can build, explore, and survive in a world made entirely of blocks. The main idea is letting players interact with the different cubes they can find, each representing a different material (dirt, stone, water, etc), by either destroying them or using them to craft new materials, tools, structures or entire landscapes. It offers two base game modes at launch: Survival mode, with a focus on gathering resources, managing health and hunger, and Creative mode, which intends to allow building freely with unlimited resources and no constraints like danger or hunger. Other variants of those can be found as well but in general the game modes change the goal of interacting with the blocks, not the core idea and characteristics of the gameplay.
The reason this game was chosen as a case study for this research is its peculiar approach to texturing. Many award-games (such as The Last of Us part II - 2020, Senua’s Saga: Hellblade II - 2024, Red Dead Redemption 2 - 2018, Cyberpunk 2077 - 2020, Horizon Forbidden West - 2022) prizing themselves for having the most realistic, cinematographic graphics, using v-ray and a dozen different techniques to deliver the closest to reality experiences, Minecraft, with its pixelated look both in its 3D models and textures, may seem an odd choice for a project highlighting textures. Nevertheless, it has one of the strongest examples of texturing usage in video games. Minecraft materials communicate with players through visually minimal cues, which Laura Marks (2002) analyses in the similar case of the heavily pixelated images recorded by Fisher-Price PixelVision (1987): « Pixelvision, the format of the discontinued Fisher-Price toy camera that used audiocassette tapes, is an ideal haptic medium. Pixelvision, which cannot focus on objects in depth, gives a curious attention to objects and surfaces in extreme close-up. Thus some of the best Pixelvision works focus on scenes of detail. ». Minecraft explores a similar logic by playing with numerous recombinations of a few pixels, making a slight color change in one of them relevant to the game. Also, each material in the game is a multi-behavior-driven surface, containing various synesthetic reactions to the player’s touch. 
In the case of videogames, the word Texture refers to the visuals applied to all game objects, characters, effects, inventory and even the environment; it allows players to recognize them. As shown in the image on the next page, removing the textures from Minecraft would turn the game not only into a sad and boring grayish-looking prototype with lacking depth (as it would no doubt do with any game) but actually render the whole experience pretty much unplayable. Texturing is an extremely important affordance in Minecraft as most blocks in game have nothing else to differentiate themselves. As so, it influences almost all of a players choices: which blocks to destroy and which to ignore, where to plant your food, how to build a house, what to choose from your inventory, how to craft new items, and the split-second decision about whether the form walking in your direction is a peaceful villager or a zombie.
The game counts an overwhelming amount of approximately 2,300 distinct textures (version Java 1.21, 2025) - a number that keeps increasing as players are able to develop and share new packs of customized materials. Considering that the standardized format for these square images in the game is only 16×16 pixels, the extent of detail and variation achieved with such limited options makes the number of textures even more impressive. That quantity of deviations albeit, counts only the superficial cosmetic changes applied in game.
As seen in the previous chapter, the sense of touch does not rely solely on a single property to identify a texture. The senses are often interconnected and supply cues to one another to enrich interactions. In the case of Minecraft (and video games, in general) these can be found in the form of audio, visual and behavior feedback. A block being walked onto will produce a specific sound, defining to which group of blocks it belongs to; one being hit by a player will sound, produce particles and, sometimes, spawn an item; a block of water can be passed through, both producing sound and changing physics at the same time (speed of the player decreases and it gains the ability to move up and down). 
Taking this holistic approach to Touch, Minecraft textures can be spread into two broad categories of purely decorative (look different but behave the same) or behavior-linked (associated with distinct logic, mechanics, or interactivity). In the first case, the textures (and objects they are applied to, by extension) look different but behave identically - for example, the 16 colored terracotta blocks, 8 wood types, glazed terracotta variants, banners, carpets, etc. This category constitutes roughly from 2,050 to 2,000 of Minecraft textures, meaning only a small percentage of the textures are able to be identified through their reaction to the game environment or player, instead of depending on their visual cues only. In the second case (around 10–15% of the game textures), the textures are associated with distinct logic, mechanics, or interactivity, organizing themselves into behavior classes. Five from those materials, from different texture families and behavior groups, were chosen to serve as examples of the materiality diversity explored in Minecraft.
[image: Minecraft graphic of behavior classes]

BLOCKS
Those textures represent the structural layer of the game, forming the environment and determining available physical interactions, placements, and collisions; they define the appearance of the world’s building materials and terrain. Although mainly static, some can contain simple animations to illustrate elements' movements (e.g. water, lava, portals).
[image: Grass block]
ITEMS
Those are flat textures (meaning they do not warp around 3D objects) representing portable or craftable objects that players can hold, use, or trade. Unlike blocks, items are not part of the terrain; they exist in inventories or float in the world as functional tools or resources, often linked to crafting and player action. Most are static, with a few exceptions like the animated clock and compass. 
[image: Pickaxe]
ENTITIES
The most complex textures in Minecraft, as their maps are applied to dynamic models such as animals, monsters, and NPCs. Their resolutions are higher (usually following multiples of 16px) and adapted to the entity’s 3D form, often combining several body parts, projected in a single image. Entity textures are not limited to a single static image — they often include or swap between multiple textures to reflect behavioral changes such as mood, health, action, or environment.
[image: Villager]
PARTICLES
Short-lived 2D textures used for visual effects such as smoke, fire, rain, or explosions. It's common for this group of textures to have the distinct behavior of constantly rotating, making sure to always face the player in order not to reveal their un-thickness. They are mostly well known for their brief animations: varying position, size and rotation, before disappearing. Particle textures express ephemeral events and are used to emphasize an atmosphere, a touch or a motion.
[image: block breaking / digging particle]
GRAPHICAL USER INTERFACE (GUI)
Interface textures that organize the available interactions between a player and the game’s logic and storage systems (inventories). They include menus, slots, and icons for crafting, equipping, or managing items. The GUI textures work to make the systems of control and exchange with the users clear to understand and interact.

APPLE LIQUID GLASS
Liquid Glass (2025) is a software design language developed by Apple to uniformize the visual and tactile experience of its interface across platforms. Rather than a static visual style, it works as a dynamic, glass-like material that adapts to light, motion, and context by refracting and reflecting its surroundings in real time, flexing under touch, and shifting its tint and depth to maintain clarity and focus. The idea behind it was to create an interface that could seamlessly blend with the real world while augmenting it, as a new living and responsive material co-inhabiting our environment.
Apple’s UI was chosen as an example for this chapter thanks to this particular way of re-thinking our exchanges with materials and its behaviors. Contrary to Minecraft, where textures play a role of showcasing the great variety of possible interactions open to the player, Liquid Glass does, in fact, quite the opposite. Uniformizing the whole IOS system interface to a shiny and translucent look, it seems at first glance to deprive us from the multiple textural cues that usually help guide our interactions with gadgets. It compensates by offering instead a more subtle and tactual approach to our screens. In Apple's UX designers own words:
"(...) rather than trying to simply recreate a material from the physical world, Liquid Glass is a new digital meta-material that dynamically bends and shapes light. And simultaneously it behaves and moves organically in a manner that feels more like a lightweight liquid, responding to both the fluidity of touch and the dynamism of modern apps." 
(Chan, designer on Apple’s Human Interface Design team, in Apple Developer, 2025, 0:41–1:04)
The majority of our current interactions with the digital take place through touch-screens. They are, as the name suggests, designed for touch, and their system UI’s are responsible to provide us with different tactile feedback to reward us for our interactions. The use of Textures in this scenario, referring to the visual elements that allow us to communicate and control our devices and apps, represents only one dimension of those interactions. While graphics may play with depth, surface detail, and variation in color and lighting, all of which make digital elements feel more tangible, easier to identify, and state interface hierarchies; auditory, and tactile cues follow closely in UI. They interweave with them in order to provide clear feedback for our actions. For example, a button might have a subtle gradient or shadow to make it look raised and clickable; it might also slightly change its color, while emitting a soft, crisp click sound and a quick, subtle vibration when interacted with.
With UI (as seen with games) touch is thought in a more systemic way, bringing together different sensorium cues in order to build the simulation of tactile interactions. The logic guiding this medium however, is usually that of one element, linked to one behavior that declenches a response through multi-sensorium reactions. Taking the previous example: a button awaits the solely act of being clicked, in response to which it will activate sound, vibration and color shifting. That said, as seen in the Minecraft example, in video games it is not that rare to find materials embedded with multiple behaviors—that go beyond simple one-action = multiple outputs logic. If we take Minecraft water as an example, we have a component that behaves as a complex, multi-systemic material: it has a flow behavior, that makes it react by spreading itself following gravity and near blocks shapes; a state behavior, that reacts by changing the material properties as freezing into ice or turning into obsidian or cobblestone when meeting lava. It has also an environmental behavior, that reacts by extinguishing fires, pushing entities, and carrying items; an ecosystem behavior, that hosts fish, kelp and seagrass and a player behavior, modifying its breathing, buoyancy, and visibility.
That sort of complex, multi-behavior materials are not rare nowadays in games, but having them in interfaces mediating ordinary, practical, everyday needs, it’s quite exceptional. Apple’s Liquid Glass is built out of one single texture with multiple behaviours such as flexing, changing color and dissolving backgrounds, imbued to it; also, the way to unlock those behaviours is by touching the material with different gestures or patterns. It reverses the idea of textures being used to provide differentiation/identity to digital objects or cues on the environment and disponible actions to instead transfer those abilities to the user itself. Its touch and intention shapes the object, gives it identity and creates new interactions, like when a longer touch detaches a switch button from its layer and brings it forward to signal it is possible to change its state, as if it was literally being picked up. As one of the system beta testers testifies: “I've been testing this on my iPhone (...), and I keep catching myself just... touching things to watch them respond.” (Fateh_Alam_Bhatty,https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/my-take-apples-liquid-glass-fateh-alam-bhatty-nqdlf).
Liquid Glass counts a number of 8 different behaviors associated to its single texture: 
Morphing behavior: Liquid Glass responds to user interaction by flexing, rippling, and morphing in real time. Elements can lift temporarily when touched, giving the interface a sense of fluidity and responsiveness.
Lensing behavior: Objects materialize or fade by modulating light bending rather than simply appearing/disappearing, preserving optical integrity. Liquid Glass bends and concentrates light to communicate presence, motion, and layering, giving a sense of depth while letting content shine through.
State behavior: Controls maintain a floating plane and morph smoothly as users navigate between app sections, ensuring fluid, seamless transitions.
Shifting modes behavior: adapts light/dark modes depending on content behind it.
Scale behavior: adapts elements size, according to content and gesture (scrolling). As elements grow in size (e.g., sidebars), the material appears more substantial with deeper shadows, richer lensing, and softer light scattering.
Tint behavior: adjust elements colors depending on background content/contrast. Adjusts hue, brightness, and saturation based on underlying content to emphasize primary elements or maintain visual harmony.
Blur behavior: Dissolve content into the background when scrolled underneath higher hierarchy elements.
Highlight behavior: Responds to environmental light, touch, and device motion. Highlights move dynamically, define silhouettes, and illuminate from beneath touch points. Shadows adapt to the content and help ground elements visually. - when interacting with liquid glass, the material illuminates from within, as a form of feedback. Glow starts from beneath the fingertip and spreads onto any liquidGlass elements nearby.

IKEA KREATIV
IKEA Kreativ (2022) is a tool launched by IKEA to let their clients digitally plan, visualize and shop for furniture. The experience starts with the creation of a 3D digital space, where users can choose from an existing showroom or build their own room through a mix of picture and space scan. The visuals and depth information collected are then analyzed by computer vision algorithms in order to detect walls, floor planes, furniture shapes, lighting and perspective; building something similar to a digital copy of it. After these base steps, the new digital room becomes directly edible: users can select the objects they would like to remove from the space and “clean it“ through AI Inpainting, a technique in which the pixels belonging to each object are recognized and covered by a masking layer of generative painting, reconstructing hidden parts of the background. Once the digital model of the room is ready, the last step is to place IKEAs products in it to see how they fit and look together. The furniture models use the previous computer calculations to sit realistically on floors and stop at walls, correct the brightness and shadows cast over them and scale the digital objects to correct proportions. Users can still rotate, move or replace objects to see other options or different colors and patterns from its variations. 
This application was chosen as the last case study of this research in order to bring a digital twin perception on the usage of digital textures and touch. It will also serve to bridge our way into the last chapter, which will approach tactile interfaces to touch the digital. Digital twins can be defined as digital copies of a real-world object, entity or system, containing both its visuals and its properties while synchronizing data between them. It is mainly used for digital simulations of a real world application, such as: cities and buildings twins (simulate energy, transport, lightning), cars and aircraft twins (simulate speed, fuel consumption, engine health, components temperature), design twins (simulate materials behaviors, object usage, patterns and shapes compositions), among others. This field plays an important position in the realm of digital textures as it works closely with digital and physical materials, constantly having to translate them across these environments. As far as it goes though, most of these processes remain restricted to the design and production backstages, still rarely making their way to the general public (which takes place mostly in the form of digital try-ons). As the interest of this thesis remains in studying interactions built for broad audiences, IKEAs Kreativ approachable digital twins and physical products make it possible to analyze how those two are perceived and compared by users.
The term texture when referring to digital twins and digital prototyping (the digital simulations of models before their physical production) have some particular properties. It can refer, as in video games, to the images applied onto each 3D product surface, giving its color and material appearance. It can also mean, in the cases where virtual try-ons are used, the visuals and lighting of real-world objects, spaces or people the digital twins interact with through cameras and pictures. At last, taking into consideration they also deal with the physical reality, it links back as well to the physical and tactile definition of texture as “the quality of something that can be decided by touch, for example whether it is rough or smooth, or soft or hard“ CAMBRIDGE UNIVERSITY PRESS & ASSESSMENT, 2025. “Texture” [online]. [Accessed 19 October 2025]. Available at: https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/texture. In Kreativ case, this corresponds to the images applied to the 3D furniture surface, the scanned picture deployed to create the digital version of the room and its masking layers, used to simulate the background visuals, as well as the actual physical materials employed to produce the physical twin of the simulated products.
In that way, IKEA Kreativ states as well yet another way of dealing with textures and touch. Where Minecraft example contained thousands of different textures, and reproduced hundreds of behaviours, and Liquid Glass worked with one texture that behaved in 8 distinct ways; Kreativ includes a huge library of textures, digitalizing most of the physical materials contained in IKEA catalogue as well as bringing clients own rooms textures into the game. But those do not contain behaviors  intertwined with them, apart from the brightness and shadows casted over the digital objects that get inserted in the space. When we consider that this experience is supposed to come in hand with the user's physical interaction with the same products, something seems to be missing. 
A possible reason for such a choice could be that, since engaging with a digital twin suggests at some point a touch stage with the actual physical product, building a complex digital material with multiple tactual behaviors could prove itself hard and risky. Users may have different sensorium interpretations of those and consider the comparison between both twins to be deceiving. Although a valid position, it is important to remember that many clients already return products for the same reason, because visuals-only can be very misleading if you plan on later engaging with a product in full senses.
Another probable cause could be that, as mentioned by Frederic Frache during an interview about his experience with digital twins in Decathlon, the link between digital and physical twins is sometime very hard and subtle to be kept:
When you’re working with physical and digital products or components, one is always lagging behind the other. It’s a tremendous effort to keep the digital twin up to date to the physicality of what you're doing on your prototype, for example, or later when you arrive at the development phase of your product. At some point, both of them are going to separate a little bit. And in Decathlon, that's going to be at the moment you are going through production, because the supplier is going to make small arrangements because it's easier to produce, or the machine is not able to do that, or for efficiency reasons during the production. 
Basically, I think the biggest difference between physical and digital, and with digital twins especially, is imperfection. A fabric is, by nature, imperfect; some way more than others. But the problem with digital is that it is perfect. There's no soul in the material that you are creating in 3D. And when you put that on an already perfect 3D model, it becomes something really cold and precise. You lose a lot of what we're used to seeing in the real world, small imperfections that our brain is easing off and blurring off, but that makes a connection between what we're looking at and what we know it's real. Making it perfect means you add a lot of defects. And it's quite difficult. Sometimes, you have to spend way more time adding defects into a material than making it. Being realistic  in a digital space is too subtle.
What I think is really lost during the process of digitizing something is all the other senses apart from sight. Sound, touch, dryness, sometimes the smell also. Today, you're going to be stressing a lot or pushing a little bit some aspect of the fabric, for example, to make it more fuzzy than it is to convey the idea that it's super soft. You have to lie a little bit.
Whatever the reason, especially in IKEAs case, reducing their product's tactile experience to only its visual properties seems problematic. During two field observations on touch, one analyzing people's behaviors to tactile exchanges with objects in everyday activities and a second trying to document every touched surface in a day, I was able to collect interesting data corroborating that. In the first case, an interesting phenomenon was that people in general avoid touching things, if possible. Apart from some designed-to-touch surfaces (such as handrails, grab poles and door knobs) most people seem to pass through places with little or no tactual contact help, relying on sight, hearing or smell to guide them. The situation would drastically change as soon as they engaged in a shopping activity though. The act of choosing an item, no matter its category (clothing, food, furniture), to belong to you, seems to instantly requisite touch to make sure the candidate is a fitting choice. Before selecting any item, people would run their palms or fingers through them, pinch them or lightly squeeze them in their hands and evaluate their weight, temperature and general comfort to, only then, make the decision if they wanted to keep it or not. 
During the second field observation, similar to the first results, I could easily document every touched surface I came in direct contact with for half a day, while visiting multiple sites. The moment I returned home, however, the number escalated so fast that I had to give up documentation after reaching 200. Both these exercises show an interesting and inconscient approach to touch: we tend to use it in things we consider to belong to us, or to appropriate new belonging parts. That is why I consider ignoring IKEAs products' tactual properties in Kreativ as extremely limiting to clients' engagement.
It is interesting to notice that, in comparison to the other examples, what is lacking in Kreativ touch experience is not the graphic part of its 3D objects. Even when compared to Minecraft, their textures are visually much more “realistic“ and are evoquing physical materials people have a deep tactual knowledge of. The things missing in IKEA Kreativ are the holistic properties of touch: the way a material is supposed to sound, move, change and react to our touch, combining to help us to really feel it.

Chapter 3 - Augmented Touch
During this research, the one question I asked all my interviewees was what they imagined an Augmented Touch could be(1). I initially built the expression by borrowing the same structure used in Augmented Reality, wanting to start from the same notion of a technology that overlayed new layers of digital information with the goal of not replacing, but enhancing the world. An augmented touch would, in my perception, be something contained within the bigger umbrella of augmented or enhanced reality, a hybrid sensorium sandbox that could allow us to develop new interactions and perceptions with our own physical touch.
This chapter is dedicated to building this speculative notion of what an augmented touch could be. Here I start by explaining my trajectory in working with digital and physical materiality and how this expanded notion of hybrid touch evolved through my work to become something so dear to my research. Finally, through excerpts from interviews, personal observations, and examples from other research projects exploring touch as an interface, I aim to shape a possible definition of this augmented form of touch.
We conclude by resuming all these theories and rationality into a first glimpse on what kind of touchable experiences we could imagine immerse ourselves into in the future.

Growing up in 1990s Brazil meant a material and hands-on childhood. TV, and later computers, were limited to just a few hours a week, and most of the time was spent building toys from old packagings or whatever we had in hand—trying to reproduce the visual and material references of the world inside tiny shoeboxes, debating for hours whether cellophane paper had the right light, transparency, color, sound, smoothness, and temperature to evoke water. That early habit of translating the world through improvised materials and their subjectivity, followed me for years all the way to my studies in Fashion Design and Sculpture, where this tactile curiosity evolved into a deliberate practice of material training. Mornings were spent surrounded by fabrics and trims, teaching my fingers the difference between a mix of 30%, 50% or 95% in polyamide composition, and playing with weight, cuts, folds, softness and roughness combinations to represent narratives through tactile and visual cues. Afternoons were spent across wood, metal, stone, clay and resin workshops, learning about the softness and hardness of each: how to follow the lines of a wood’s grain, how to bend an iron piece, carve a stone, mold and burn clay, mix and sand resin. It was a process of learning to read matter through touch—a language I would later find myself trying to translate into digital works.
Toward the end of my studies, this material exploration expanded for the first time into digital territories when I was invited to contribute in a collective installation on humanity’s inventory, inspired by the Voyager satellite Golden Records(2). The piece displayed a mix of physical and digital-only objects, presented side-by-side through augmented reality. Being developed in 2016 and mostly due to technical constraints, the virtual models had a specific visual haptics that would set them out immediately from the other everyday objects displayed in our collection; they « felt » like plaster sculptures, the kind so familiar from our art school studios. This idea of a digital matter and materiality was highly appealing and drove me into a long series of experiments that have been continually developed since then. At this point, It might be worth noting that, even though having worked with digital media before for fashion and art illustrations and short animations, all those 2D experiences so far had evoked in me an instant connection to Painture and Gravure. Their approach to space and materiality followed the same perspective and light/shadow principles taught during drawing and painting classes - the goal was to represent space into a flat surface and not to inhabit and sculpt space and matter themselves. Even the tools used in 2D and 3D softwares stated that difference: brushes, pencil, pen, eraser, smudge, blend and stamp options for illustration/ painting programs; and sculpt, clay, pinch, flatten, scrape, smooth, polish, extrude and inset for 3D modeling programs. That’s why, being able to occupy this virtual space and explore its materials properties the same way I used to do in my sculpture workshops, spoke to me much more powerfully than any of my previous digital experiences.
My first reaction was to understand and approach 3D matter as clay. It seemed natural, starting with an initial sphere to pinch, grab and move its parts, adding or subtracting more material as needed. When finished, there was still the option to add different pigments and bake color and modifications, the same way I would burn a ceramic piece and glaze it. After getting a little more familiar with digital sculpting techniques, my experiments naturally inclined into the direction of using different shapes to carve empty spaces onto each other, similar to the way I would do with stone or wood. That got me extremely precise incisions in the matter and evoked in my imagination different materials properties then the same smooth neutral visuals applied by default to 3D models. That was when I realized that, differently from what happens in our physical world, where the material belongs intrinsically to the matter being worked with, in the digital space, material and matter are different subjects, configured independently. The realization drove me into studying digital materials more deeply, getting familiar with the notions of uv mapping, displacement, normals, weight and texture maps. When my experiments drove me to a new form of virtual manipulation, by building digital molds I could use to contain and solidify 3D matter in a resin-like manner, the use of digital materials became even more important, as multi-materiality compositions grew into a common ground of work.
That accumulated physical and digital material knowledge eventually led me to work in a digital fabrication lab specializing in fashion and technology research. The lab’s main focus was to explore how digital fabrication techniques—such as 3D modeling, texturing, 3D printing, milling, laser cutting, and electronics—could be used to imagine and construct new forms of wearables.  Over the course of two years, I was able to experiment with hybrid materiality and apply it within fashion contexts, deepening my understanding of how digital and physical landscapes translate into one another. This raised a series of new questions: What happened to materiality once it was deconstructed into digital form and then reconstructed back into the physical world? How should we conceive and combine materials when a digital designed surface is printed over a physical one—such as in 3D printing on fabric, in which the printed structure fuses directly into the fibers beneath it? I still didn’t manage to answer many of these questions, but my experiences led me to question materiality in a deeper and more subjective way.
The tests developed during this period evolved into my final thesis project, UMBRA (2020). Developed in the context of isolation imposed by COVID-19, the research proposed a reflection on the boundaries between physical and digital realities and the virtualization of daily actions by seeking to create a “daily life texture” — a hybrid surface that captured the blurred boundaries between online and offline existence. This texture was generated by overlaying multiple daily images, combining same moment stills of cellphone screenshots and camera photographs into mappings of daily-life experiences. The resulting visual layers — merging fragments of physical surroundings with digital interfaces — were interpreted as material topographies and translated into three-dimensional textile prototypes through a combination of 3D modeling, 3D printing, laser cutting and textile manipulation. In this process, fashion was used as a medium to question how presence and tactility were reconfigured within hybrid environments and how technology could be used to frame every-day life differently, building new ways of being.
As another form of materiality investigation, working on 3D experiences for marketing campaigns allowed me a deeper engagement with the digitalization of physical materials. This work involved studying digital materials across multiple media — including video games, animations, AR, VR, and installations — and provided the opportunity to extend the exploration of hybrid materials by leading digital twins projects, as the fashion film for the 2023 Inspiramais fair.  The Inspiramais Fair is a biannual Brazilian design event that showcases innovative and sustainable materials for the fashion, footwear, furniture, and accessories industries. Many of these are handcrafted, using traditional techniques like crochet, weaving and straw basketry or melting and pressing residues for recycled surfaces. Digitally recreating these richly textured materials demanded attention and precision to the modeling and texturing process. While the meticulous visual result of these digital twins optically evoked the original textures, the models fell short in terms of the material richness they were supposed to carry. The digital twins looked like the originals, but didn’t feel like them. They could not convey the tactile properties of tension, stiffness, weight and grain of the materials; nor could they transmit all the physical work artisans had invested in their creation. This experience shows the division between the richness of digital possibilities and the limitations in exploring actual materiality that is experienced by many artists and designers when dealing with hybrid materiality.
When approached about this translation exercise, fashion and product designers stated similar impressions. The interviewed professionals work mainly with digital twins, and need to constantly compare physical and digital properties of materials. For them, the freedom in creativity and the economy in ideation time are two big advantages in the use of 3D on their workflow but material simulation stands as the main reason for its adoption. Haptic feeling (even if only visual haptics) is crucial and, at the same time, underdeveloped to support full thrust on the conveyed feel of a product, reason why physical prototyping still precedes any design final approbation.  When asked about his impressions, Akihito Hira, Head of Fashion Innovation in Renner commented:
I really believe that 3D technology has brought numerous benefits, especially in terms of sustainability. It significantly reduces the need for physical samples during development. We also gain a certain predictability regarding what will be produced. I think it helps a lot both in the development stage and in the creative stage — which is where design really comes in. 
From my experiences, I’ve always worked using surface scanners or high-quality, high-resolution photographs. For example, I used X-Rite at the time to capture material appearances. I also used software like Clo3D or Style3D to calibrate mechanical behavior — because it’s essential not only to capture the visual aspect but also the physical, mechanical properties of the fabric. It’s a very empirical process — there’s a technical side but also a sensory one. I think even the practice of tailoring plays a role here, because you have to observe, test, and adjust these behaviors with millimetric precision. So, I believe that a professional who works with raw materials, textures, and pattern making needs to be very attentive and hands-on to truly understand the material’s behavior.
Now, during material translation, I think that, first of all, we lose the unpredictability of the material itself. The friction and, especially, the immediate response of the fabric — that sense of manipulating it, touching it with your hands — are also lost. But I think we gain a lot too; we gain a new layer of control and experimentation. We can manipulate physical properties, test behaviors, or even imagine materials that don’t yet exist. I believe this notion of digital touch doesn’t replace the physical one in any way. The key word here, I think, is that it expands and reinterprets all of this. So, through that, I think we’re able to explore new possibilities within the digital realm. 
Gabriela Scanferla, 3D Creative Lead and Project Manager at Metakosmos, complement on her experience with virtual try-ons for accessories and beauty products:
In the digital realm we can handle the visual aspect quite well, right? We can create something visual, or even include sound, to convey what the target of that product or message is. But the experience itself — wow — it’s very subjective, and that’s difficult. I think with touch, for instance, we can feel texture. And that’s something really hard to put into words or to describe — like how a product feels on the skin, whether it’s a bit drier, more emollient, or soft. We lose those qualities and have to simply describe what it looks like in order to move forward. So I always ask my clients for visual references of that subjective impression they are trying to pass, because through those images I can understand what needs to be done to deliver that visual aspect, and then work with height, light, and shadow to try to represent that sense of texture visually.
Experimenting with such notions of expanding and reinterpreting materiality, the project LÉMAN PULSE (2024), addresses texture and space in a more holistic way with the intention of creating a straighter connection between matter, material and embodiment. The project began as a field investigation into how people communicate through visible and invisible layers of information and mapped the material and virtual landscape formed by those around lake Léman in Geneva. The shores of the lake were documented using 360° video, audio recordings, photographic observations, interviews, measurements of electromagnetic activity (EM), Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and radio frequencies. This collection of human, environmental, and digital traces was then transformed into an interactive 3D representation of the lake's main points, where the geography was dynamically distorted by the user proximity (zoom in into the space) and the intensity of EM waves recorded in the physical locations. Users could navigate these altered landmarks while listening to the soundscape of original sounds, EM interference, and stories shared by interviewees, adding memory, sound and impressions of movements and communication into the visuals of these sensory islands. 
This subjective approach to materiality, which not only engages other senses, but also builds connections with memory, seems somehow to bring us closer to the feeling of embodiment. Camille De Dieu talks about a similar perception in works developed by her and Laurent Novac at Z1 Studio, in which the cues used in installation's scenography served not to reproduce every detail realistically, but to leave space for the evoked feeling to be called forward by memory and imagination:
We worked on a research project about how to work around melting snow. Because typically, snow is something that will disappear — and how will we be able to talk about that to future generations, how will we be able to touch snow that no longer exists? So there was an entire work around archiving photos and ceramics. The idea was to create archives of ice, to have ice-like slabs. And to turn that into digital it’s difficult because it`s ephemeral. But by going through something tangible, like ceramics, we can then imagine, within the digital, a world where it becomes anchored; which means we don’t necessarily need a physical approach. Texture is what still brings a certain humanity to what exists in 3D, in the digital world. It reminds us of memories, makes us think of something else. And so, it inevitably evokes different kinds of softness as well. 
We can imagine these tactile affordances we give in interactive experiences as being a bit like those childhood games where you put lots of objects under a cloth and have to guess what they are. I think there’s something like that going on. We don’t really care about the shape of the object. I don’t know if the object really needs to have a realistic form — from my point of view, I don’t think that’s necessary. It’s more about having, through your touch, something imagined. I wonder if texture can be purely digital. I don’t think so. Because before I touch something, I don’t really sense its possibilities — what it could be through my memories.
The attraction that digital plays is precisely that of bringing new contrasts to our embodied perception, as it offers us a sandbox to play with our own senses, while allowing us to attach the sensations we want to link it to and design new connections - new forms of feeling and communicating with other people. In Layers of Understanding - L.O.U. (2024), a soft robot that acts as a textured floor surface, a new speculative relationship is developed between residents and their home. LOU is a surface that unfolds in different shapes depending on how people approach it: it can be walked over, but only if it allows you to; it can be petted or caressed, if you stay aware of how you move around it; It can block your path and make you redo your steps if you startle it. This design research took a step back from the purely digital representation to focus on the notion of skin and touch as an interface between us and our domestic spaces. The exploration started by documented and reimagined everyday household objects as living “skin species” — entities with their own textures, rhythms, and behaviors. Each was described through its tactile and emotional qualities — roughness, fragility, warmth, or adaptability — and its capacity to communicate with other species and environments. The project also mapped the different textures found in the body and the different gestures that evoked the notion of skin. By the end, multiple patterns were cutted, inspired by the different body textures and exhaustively tested in order to evoke in the public the desire of touching - caressing their fingers over the robot surface. The result was a piece that played with space delimitation, movement (both of the robot and the ones interacting with it), and longing for connection.
In some ways, LOU has many qualities central to the notion of Augmented Touch I’m trying to grasp, even though it doesn’t show a direct exchange between digital and physical environments. For firsts, it puts spatial exploration in the foreground, making us conscious again of our own body and the way we move - in a certain way, it embodies us to the physical reality. It also invites and reacts to touch, creating a form of tactile communication that resorts a lot in memory to draw associations with known motor habits and propose ways of engaging with this new entity/material. I would like to briefly mention two other research projects that seem to build a similar logic of digital touch. Although more technical than the other examples viewed so far, they also focus on the interactivity between digital matter, material, body and space and offer interesting views on the possibilities of rendering digital materials through touch.
In FeelPen (2023), Bence L. Kodak and Yasemin Vardar, propose a haptic stylus capable of rendering tactile properties of images. It integrates three main haptic modules (electrovibration system, voice-coil actuator and a peltier element) to simulate roughness and friction (through vibration), stiffness (through a resisting, pushing-back force) and temperature (through warm and cold sensations), which are called upon as the user explore the screen-space of digital interfaces, softwares or games. Pressing the pen onto a touchscreen feels like pressing into rubber, plastic or wood; it can identify if a surface is soft, stiff, elastic, smooth, slippery, bumpy, warm or cold. Among the applications of the technology, the authors specifically mention the creative potential of digital materials creation, empowering designers and artists to sculpt with texture itself.
The Materiable project (2016), developed in MIT Media Lab, on the other hand, presents a TUI (touchable user interface)(3) that aims to represent dynamic material properties using pin-based shapes. It integrates different sensation techniques, including haptics and visual feedback, to create a model of digital matter itself: a deformable and dynamic material whose properties change and respond to touch, reproducing the properties of flexibility, elasticity and viscosity. When invited to interact with it, participants naturally used not only their hands but other parts of their body and were able to identify the rendered material properties, describing the high elasticity solid as "trampoline material" and the low dampening liquid as "water", focusing the descriptions on what they felt while describing the simulation, even when instructed to focus on visual observation. Materiable takes a step further into hybrid materiality by blending the properties of digital matter and material into something that is neither a physical material nor a digital one, but almost an independent being in between.
Those two examples speak powerfully to me probably because of my background in materiality translation. Materiable brings back the same questions I first encountered in my experiments in the digital fabrication lab, while trying to turn into physical, digitally native designs. While FeelPen seems to stand somewhere in between the digital twins material translation and my first digital sculpting tests, trying to apply traditional artistic techniques to digital matter. I believe that their shared ability to render digital properties into physical space holds essential value for the idea of augmented touch, as it defines a dynamic link between the possibilities of digital materiality and the perception of physical properties.

It’s also important to understand and filter this touching aspect in digital augmented touch, as not everything we touch in the physical world is meant to have its solidity directly translated to our digital and hybrid experiences. As stated by Cyril, Head QA of Adobe Substance, “If it's cumbersome, people won’t use it. The first thing is that you need to think of a useful application for that haptic feedback. Because like, feeling a product from Amazon? I don`t think that’s a useful occasion to use that technology. Second thing, you have to make it easy to access and not burdensome to use. I think it needs to bring a useful experience to the user”. The same position was supported by Holy New, as she defended the use of touch in digital experiences as a new realm of possibilities: “I genuinely think that, if it's not adding to the experience, then it shouldn't be in there. When I'm designing, for example, I don't necessarily want to be touching a mannequin and want to be able to move through it. And sometimes that's what you need to be able to do. You need to be able to go into a mesh. Also, when I touch a digital object, what is it that I’m feeling? Is it its weight? Its texture? Can I be touching and feeling its color? Is it blue warm or is it blue cold? At that point we begin to develop a new language and then, in order to be able to communicate through it, we need to choose the right time and place for placing touch.”
Taking all the possibilities, examples, cues and considerations grouped into this chapter, I believe we can set, if not a perfectly well defined notion of it, at least the contours of an augmented touch design definition. The first point, as we are coming from a concept based on the notion of touch itself; is defining it as a design linked to spatial exploration. The second and third points are that it must evoke in the user a notion of embodiment shared between digital and physical dimensions at once and anchored itself in the sense of touch as the interface to navigate through both spaces. The fourth and most important point is that it should be able to dynamically render physical properties of materials into digital information and digital properties into physical sensations. This means not only the visuals, but also the properties inherent to touch, hearing, smell and taste. It doesn’t need to encompass all and every property at the same time, but, as fifth point, an augmented touch must be able to work with dynamic synesthesia in order to allow new memory associations and creative reconfigurations. This is also what would make it possible for the user and design piece to communicate, developing a shared alphabet of motor habits and abstract ideas.
