“Liquid Glass” signals a deeper shift for us, interface/UX/product designers and for developers as well. We’ve long treated design systems as collections of UI patterns, components, guidelines, fonts, and sometimes animations. But Apple shows that consistency isn’t just about alignment and spacing anymore.

It begins with consistency of the material itself — its responsiveness, its viscosity, its behavior under light and pressure. In Liquid Glass, material consistency becomes the basis for design consistency. The way the interface flexes, reacts, and adapts becomes part of the unified design language — not just how it looks, but how it behaves and responds.

Liquid Glass isn’t just rendered once — it’s computed continuously. It dynamically adapts to light, content, and context. It’s live, real-time response to surroundings and user input. The material flexes, refracts, and reacts ; its every presence calculated across multiple layers: highlights, shadows, tint, luminance, and surface graphics.

This changes the game: interfaces are no longer just layouts. They’re behaviors. Designers and developers are no longer working on static interface elements— we’re shaping real-time, responsive matter.

At first glance, the glassy interface might seem like just another visual layer. But try to replicate it and you’ll quickly realize the scale of the challenge. One Android developer who attempted to recreate the effect shared this:

“I honestly don’t know how Apple pulled it off. After trying to mimic it (using AGSL shaders — author’s note), I’ve gained a whole new respect for Liquid Glass. My guess is they’re using shaders too — just far more refined. Apple has spent years building a system that makes this possible. Android isn’t quite there yet. Maybe Xiaomi or another Chinese brand will figure out a workaround. But until we get an open-source solution, true glassmorphism on Android remains out of reach.”

This isn’t just eye-catching animation — it’s a coordinated system of material behaviors, lighting, touch feedback, and real-time shaders. Liquid Glass demands power, precision… and ideally a 120Hz ProMotion display to truly shine.

## Key Characteristics:
### Dynamic & Translucent: 
Liquid Glass is a translucent material that shifts and changes its appearance based on the content behind it, moving from light to dark to maintain clarity and legibility. 
Refraction & Reflection: The material refracts light, creating a sense of depth and a lively, "wet" appearance, according to Apple. 

Controlers refract the content behind them — while reflecting content and the user’s wallpaper from around them — which ensures users always have a sense of their context.

### Expressive & Delightful: 
Apple describes the design as "expressive, delightful, but still instantly familiar," aiming to create a more vibrant and engaging user experience. 

### Unified Design Language:
Liquid Glass provides a consistent visual theme across all Apple operating systems, from iPhones and iPads to Macs and Apple Watches. 

Perfectly concentric fit with the rounded corners of modern hardware and app windows — establishing greater harmony between hardware, software, and content.

Controls are crafted as a distinct functional layer that sits above apps. 

### Dynamic Motion: 
The design incorporates real-time rendering to create a visually rich experience, with specular highlights and dynamic responses to user movement. 

Controlers give way to content and dynamically morph as users need more options or move between different parts of an app. And with thoughtful groupings, it’s easier for users to find the controls they need.

when users scroll, tab bars shrink to bring focus to the content while keeping navigation instantly accessible. The moment users scroll back up, tab bars fluidly expand. 

### Usage:
**App Icons and Widgets:** Crafted from multiple layers of Liquid Glass. 
**Controls and Navigation:** Elements like tab bars and sidebars are designed with the Liquid Glass aesthetic. 
**Lock Screen:** The lock screen distorts content and reflects light, providing a unique visual effect. 

This is used across iOS elements like buttons, sliders, media controls, switches, notifications, tab bars, app sidebars, and in system experiences like the Control Center, Home Screen, Lock Screen, and more.

Extends from the smallest elements users interact with every day — like buttons, switches, sliders, text, and media controls — to larger elements, including tab bars and sidebars for navigating apps. It also shines in system experiences, such as the Lock Screen, Home Screen, notifications, Control Center, and more.

Apple has addressed this by adjusting the Liquid Glass effect to maintain text clarity, regardless of what’s displayed underneath. For instance, in apps like Photos or Music, tab labels now stand out crisply, even when scrolling over vibrant album art or complex visuals.

"Picture this: every single interface element on your phone—buttons, menus, even your damn text messages—now looks like it's made of this translucent, light-refracting glass. It shifts and adapts based on what's behind it, ripples when you touch it, and somehow manages to look both futuristic and nostalgic at the same time" 

> real-time ray tracing on UI elements, which is something I never thought I'd see outside of a gaming context. Your home screen dock literally becomes more or less see-through depending on your wallpaper. Tap a notification? It doesn't just appear—it ripples with light that follows actual physics.

I've been testing this on my iPhone 12 Pro Max (yea, it's an ancient device, i know), and I keep catching myself just... touching things to watch them respond. It's mesmerizing in a way that makes me feel like I'm seven years old playing with soap bubbles.

Okay, I'll admit it: unifying iOS 26, iPadOS 26, macOS Tahoe 26, watchOS 26, tvOS 26, and visionOS 26 under one design language is ambitious as hell. From a product strategy perspective, it's brilliant. Apple's playing the long game here.

But can we talk about how this feels forced in some places?

Glass effects that look gorgeous on my 65-inch Apple TV? Probably fine. Glass effects on my Apple Watch's tiny 1.9-inch screen while I'm trying to check the time during a run? Less convinced. I've been testing the watchOS beta, and some of these micro-animations feel empty, like you know what I mean... pretty to look at, but not actually useful.

Okay, the Vision Pro angle actually makes sense
This is where Apple's master plan clicked for me, and I have to grudgingly admit it's pretty smart.

Liquid Glass isn't really about making our phones prettier (though that's a nice side effect). It's about training us for spatial computing without us even realizing it. And as someone who's been experimenting with AR interfaces since getting my Vision Pro, this strategy is actually genius.

Think about it: if you're eventually going to put interfaces into 3D space where they need to blend seamlessly with the real world, you need users who are comfortable with translucent, depth-aware elements. By introducing these concepts on familiar 2D screens first, Apple is basically conducting the world's largest UX research study on spatial interface patterns.

It's actually brilliant change management. Instead of dropping completely alien interface paradigms on us when AR glasses eventually arrive, they're gradually introducing the visual vocabulary through devices we already love and trust. Smart? Absolutely. Slightly manipulative? Maybe, but I respect the game.

The complexity creep is real. We went from simple flat icons to multi-layered glass compositions with dynamic properties. The barrier to entry for indie developers and small teams just got significantly higher.

I've been living with this beta for a week, and I've already had multiple "why the hell can't I read this text" moments. The system tries to automatically adjust opacity based on background content, but it's not magic. When it works, it's great. When it doesn't, you're left squinting at your phone like you forgot your reading glasses.

Think about it, the experience for a color-blind individual or one with a visual defect will be noticeably worse than mine. All those subtle color shifts and environmental adaptations that might feel natural to me? Will often be completely invisible to them. That's not just a minor accessibility hiccup—that's a fundamental design failure that "Reduce Transparency" doesn't actually solve.

Then there's the battery situation. I'm seeing a 3-7% reduction in battery life just from prettier animations. Look, I know that doesn't sound like much, but for those of us already playing phone-battery-roulette every day, it's significant. Are shinier interfaces worth your phone dying at 6 PM instead of 8 PM?

Google's Material 3 Expressive feels way more thoughtful to me. Instead of transparency for the sake of looking cool, they're focusing on dynamic color theming and personalization that actually serves user needs. Their whole "Material You" philosophy puts user preference first, while Liquid Glass feels more like Apple deciding what looks good and telling us to deal with it.

But here's my fundamental question: does this make interfaces better for users, or just prettier for Apple's keynote videos?

I've spent the last six years designing products with one core principle: if it doesn't make the user's life easier, it doesn't belong in the interface. And I keep bumping up against that principle with Liquid Glass.

Does it help me complete tasks more efficiently? Not really. Does it reduce cognitive load? Often the opposite. Does it make technology more accessible? Definitely not.

### Dynamics
- rounded floating forms nest neatly in the rounded curves of devices hardware
- Clearly defined shapes feel easy to tap and are designed to relate to the natural geometry of our fingers so they feel friendly to touch interaction.
- Liquid Glass visual definition: **Lensing** - phenomenon happening all around us in the natural world. "Through this experience we've all gained an intuitive understanding of how the warping and bending of light of a transparent object communicates to us its presence, its motion and form.
Liquid glass uses these instinctive visual cues to provide separation and communicate layering, while allowing content shine through it.
This new material bends, shapes and concentrates light in real time, providing definition against the background while still feeling visually grounded in our experience of the natural world.
It sculpt light, making controls look super light.

- Instead of fading, liquid glass objects materialize in and out by gradually modulating the light bending and lensing, ensuring a graceful transition that preserves the optical integrity of the material. 

- How the material feels and behaves is just as important as the way it looks. From foundation, both the visuals and motion of LiquidGlass were designed as one.

- LiquidGlass responds to interaction by instantly flexing and energizing with light. This make the interface feel responsive, satisfying and alive. And it has an inherent gel-like flexibility to it that communicate its transient and malleable nature, as it moves in tandem with your interaction.
This fluidity helps the interface feel aligned with the dynamism and continually changing nature of our thinking and movement.

- Elements can even lift up into LiquidGlass temporarily, such as when you interact with a component. This lets the resting state stay visually quiet, while it comes to life on touch.

- Sense of fluidity = how material reacts to user input + environment input/changes in real time. LiquidGlass dynamically morphs between each context.

- As you go between states in an app, Liquid Glass dynamically morphs between the controls in each context. Mantaining concept of having a singular floating plane that the controls live on. Also makes transitions between sections look fluid and seamless, as the controls continually shape shift.

- LiquidGlass  changes the way apps *feel*, having the ability to bend and shape light in new ways, fused with the ability to dinamcally flex and shape shift, it makes the experience of app more organic, fluid and immersive.

### Adaptivity

- LiquidGlass is designed to be adaptive to both its size and environment.

- It is composed of multiple layers, that work together to give its unique look. Each layers continuously adapts based on what's behind it, changing its: 
    - amount of tint
    - casted shadows (become more or less prominent to create additional separation from content)
    - dynamic range
    - change between dark and light modes

- When glass flexes and morphs to larger sizes, its material characteristics change to simulate a thicker, more substantial material (richer, deeper shadows, more pronounced lensing and refraction effects and a softer scattering of light).
- LiquidGlass forms a distinct control layer, that flow above content.

- On larger elements like sidebars, the apperance of Liquid Glass changes with the ambient environment within the app: light from colorful content nearby can subtly spill into its surface and shadows, reinforcing the material context and its sense of elevation within the interface.

**Scroll Edge Effects**
- as the content scrolls underneath a glass element, it dissolves into the background, lifting the glass visually to the first plane (allow floating elements- like titles - to always remain clear).

- When darker content scrolls underneath the glass elements, it triggers the glass itself to transition to dark mode.

- pined accessory views under a toolbar (such as column headers), a "hard style" instead is applied, applying the effect uniformily across the heigh of the toolbar, instead of using the nomar fade (extra degree of separation - floating elements x scrolling content bellow).

### Principles

- Light play + depth effect + adaptive changes = 1 single feature (layers inside a sofisticated system working together to create a material)

#### Layers:
**Hightlights Layer**
- Liquid Glass lives in an environment
- light sources inside this environment shine on the material, producing hightlights that respond to geometry.
- On touch interaction, these lights move in space, causing light to travel around the material, defining its silhouette.
- In some cases, light respond to device motion, making it feel that liquidGlass is aware of its position in the real world.
- Shadows also play an important role in making elements feel grounded and defined (opacity shadow inccreases when over text and decreases wuen in solid light background).

- when interacting with liquid glass, the material illuminates from within, as a form of feedback.
    - glow start from beneath you fingertip and spreads onto any liquidGlass elements nearby.
- There are times when multiple layers adapt together to maintain clarity and focus within the UI hierarchy.

Goal: make the interface look alive and directely connected to the physical world and device input.

**Tint Layer**
- choosing a color, select a range of tones that are mapped to content brightness underneath the tinted element (glass in the physical world changes its hue, brightness and saturation depending on whats behind).

It is also possible to use custom colors, but should be used selectively. Items/elements with a distinct functional purpose can be tint, to bring attention.

Tinting should only be used to bring emphasis to primary elements and actions in the UI (avoid tinting all LG elements - in that case, is better to tint the content layer).

**Content Layer** 
Main information layer, positioning itself under the rest.

### Where to use liquid glass:
- it is best reserved for the navigation layer that floats above the content of the apps.

- Always avoid glass-on-glass, as stacking glass elements on top of each other can quickly make the interface feel cluttered an confusing.

- When placing elements on top of liquid glass, avois applying the material to both layers. Instead, use fills, transparency and vibrancy for the top elements to make them fill like a thin overlay that is part of the material.

**Liquid Glass variants**
LG variants should never be mixed, as they have their own characteristics and specific use cases.

- Regular
Most versatile: give all the visuals and adaptive effects while providing legibility, regadless of context. Works on any size, over any content and anything can be placed on top of it.

- Clear
Does not have adaptive behaviors. It is permantly more transparent. Should only be used when these 3 conditions are met:
    - media rich content
    - content would not be negatively affected by introducing a diming layer to it
    - Content above liquid glass is bright and bold

### Elements:
- Small elements (navbars/tabbars) - constantly adapt their appearance depending on what's behind them (also flip from light to dark depending on the background).
- Bigger elements (menus/sidebars) - adapt based on context but don't flip from light to dark (area too big - transition would be distracting). 
- Symbols/Gliphs (on top of LG) - adapt based on context, flip from light to dark to maximize contrast.


### Modifiers
- You can apply modifiers to the materials, to change certain layers of liquid glass:
    * *Reduce Transparency*: makes LG frostier, obscurring more of the content behind it.
    * Increased Constrast: makes elements predominantly black or white and hightlights them with a contrasting border
    * Reduce Motion: decreases the intensity of some effects, disabling elastic properties of the material.
