# TEXTURES

Textures in IKEA Kreativ are digital materials created to mimic materials used on physical products. It aims to blur the boundary between browsing/visualization and purchase by enabling texture / material swapping / visual customization, making those choices more immediate and less speculative.

The app combines 3D, AR, AI, and computer vision to let customers:

1. Explore IKEA products in inspirational 3D showrooms (move, swap, rotate, stack, hang IKEA products), trying different combinations in lifelike spatial settings. 

2. Scan their own rooms (via photographs) to produce an editable 3D replica of the space (accurate in scale, orientation, lighting) into which users can place IKEA furnishings, erase existing furniture, and test alternatives. 

3. Save designs to cloud, share, and carry them through the shopping journey (e.g. bring virtual layout to store) 

4. Use AI / preference learning to refine suggestions over time (Kreativ is said to adapt to users’ aesthetic preferences).

## Texture list 
- finishes swaping: specially with larger pieces/covering areas that hold strong visual effect across the room
- In surveys / reviews of IKEA’s AR efforts, the value of swapping finishes / textures is often highlighted (users like being able to visualize alternate colors/finishes). E.g. in blog coverage of Kreativ, the “swap” feature is repeatedly mentioned. (Medium article on Kreativ)
- Also, in industry commentary, the ability to let users customize finishes in real-time is seen as one of the main levers of value in immersive product experiences. (Enhance XR commentary)

### Fabric / upholstery textures (textiles) 
Among the most salient when swapping materials in AR / 3D. Users are likely to want to test color / fabric choice for sofas, chairs, cushions. The visual difference is immediately legible, and such swaps are lower-risk (less structural change). Many configurators and AR apps include multiple fabric variants as standard.

### Surface materials / finishes (wood tones, laminates, painted surfaces)
Users often compare, for instance, light wood vs dark wood vs white surfaces in the same piece of furniture or set of pieces. Because ambient light, contrast, and relative color harmony matter, this is a natural axis of variation in AR.

### Rugs / floor textures / patterns
Pattern, texture, color of floor coverings are highly visible across the room; swapping rug textures (flat weave, tufted, patterned) likely yields high engagement because the effect is holistic.

### High-texture / “tactile-evoking” materials
Materials such as velvet, boucle, wool, jute, boucle textures (textured fabrics) might attract user attention because they evoke a tactile experience—users may be curious how “soft / rich” a material will look. However, many AR renderings struggle to faithfully reproduce micro-surface detail (grain, fiber, bump) in a way that convincingly conveys tactility.

### Accent / decorative materials
(e.g. pillows, throws, small décor) may see less time per item but may be experimentally used by users wanting to test accent colors or texture contrast.


## Users Opinions:

### Positive perceptions / benefits:

- Users appreciate the ability to preview alternate textures / finishes / color schemes in situ, reducing the risk of mismatch. This leads to greater confidence in purchasing decisions.

- The “try before you buy” feel is often cited: being able to see “what if I changed this sofa to that color” in one click is appealing.

- It allows more experimentation without cost or physical effort (no need to visit multiple showrooms, request multiple fabric swatches, etc.).

- Some users report that this helps streamline decision-making and reduce buyer anxiety, especially for large / expensive items.

- The sharing / social aspect can be enhanced: users can share views of their room with different fabric / finish variations to get input from family or friends.

### Constraints / negative / cautious perceptions:

- Texture realism is limited: microsurface detail, reflections, conditional lighting, shadows, subtle irregularities are not always convincingly rendered in AR / 3D, which can reduce trust when the user gets the real object.

- The lack of touch / haptic feedback is a major gap. No AR system (yet) fully simulates the feel—thickness, weave, softness, tactile weight. This is repeatedly noted as a core limitation.

- Device performance / rendering constraints: on lower-end devices, switching textures or maintaining high-fidelity materials may degrade performance (frame rate, loading times), which can hamper the user experience.

- Cognitive overload / choice fatigue: offering too many texture/finish options may overwhelm users; there is a balance between richness of choice and decisiveness.

- Calibration / color accuracy issues: ambient light, camera calibration, and display variance may cause mismatches between what is shown and what the real material looks like under actual lighting.

- Trust / skepticism: some users may not fully trust that the AR depiction is “true”; they may still want to inspect the real sample in-store.

- Loss of haptic feedback / tactileity remains a known shortcoming: AR / virtual placement can’t yet give the “feel” (softness, thickness of fabric, weave) which remains decisive in furniture and textile purchasing.
