### Quick summary of memoire:
This thesis investigates how Touch and Materiality shape embodiment across diverse digital environments, from video games to user interfaces. It argues that while digital spaces often prioritize visual input, the integration of tactual and multi-sensory cues have a great potential in embodiment interactions.

Drawing from philosophical concepts like those of Condillac and Merleau-Ponty, the research establishes touch as a primary element of perception and uses sensory ethnography as a core methodology to map how interactive elements and sensory experiences generate meaning. It moves from physical understandings of touch and texture to examine how these are translated into digital systems. The application of such theories is analysed through three distinct case studies—a video game (Minecraft), a UI design language (Liquid Glass), and a digital twin application (IKEA Kreativ). The comparison between the weakness and strengths of those is used to confirm that solely focusing on visual fidelity risks compromising multi-sensory realism.

The final section introduces the concept of Augmented Touch to define a framework for future hybrid (digital-physical) interactions. It defends designing for embodiment by overlaying digital layers onto physical materials. This can enable new connections between physical and digital materiality and open creative possibilities.
